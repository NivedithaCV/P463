Introduction

Before the study of exoplanets our solar system was the only source of data for the study of planet formation and evolution. The geometry and property of planets were inferred only from the eight planets orbiting the sun. From these planets the planetary orientations, physical and chemical properties and characteristics of orbits were studied. The similarities within these eight planets helped in formulating theories on the formation of the solar system. But the question of whether certain properties or phenomena that were observed in our solar system was caused by the laws of physics or randomness was not able to be answered before a few decades, as there were no other planetary systems. When a planet was discovered orbiting the Sun-like star 51 Pegasi in the 1990s, these and many other puzzles began to be answered. Astronomers and the general public have grown more and more interested in finding and characterizing exoplanets, and this accelerated after the launch of the Kepler spacecraft in 2009. And other more initiatives have been conducted over the past ten years to collect information that might eventually result in the discovery of more exoplanets. Presently there are more than 5000 confirmed exoplanets that were able to be detected and these exoplanets have varying properties, they have different masses, sizes, orbital periods and also the stars they orbit are of different types.For the understanding of planet distribution and the formation pattern Kepler mission has a major hand . It was able to give a large number of planet dataset from which patterns can be identified to stimulate and test theories. The work that has been done to improve these data like understanding the Kepler dataset pipeline for looking at the detection efficiency and studies from the Kepler follow-up program has increased the reliability of the statistical inferences made. The major works in statistical study have been given in the Win and Fabrichy (2015) and Zhu and Dong (2021) . Fig: 1.

In the coming sections we will look at the planet distribution and then the implications of the statistical study on the theories in planet formation and its evolution will be discussed.

Statistical study

Methods

The first inference from the data that can be obtained using statistics is the occurrence rate of the planets that gives how many planets were detected to the observed stars. The occurrence rate is given as,

$$n_p= \frac{N_{tp}}{N_{ts}}$$

where Ntp is the Total number of planets and N ts the total number of stars. The planets will be sampled in a parameter space that can differ. The fraction of the number of stars to the number of planets is given by, $$F_p = \frac{Total\:of\:planetary\:systems}{total\:of\:stars} $$ \(n_p\) and \(F_p\) will be referred from here as the frequency of planets and frequency of the planetary systems to clear any confusions. The ratio of these terms i.e, fraction of total number of planets to the planetary system gives the multiplicity For calculating the occurrence rate the likelihood based method is used, this is a probabilistically motivated method where the model is based on Poisson distribution by taking into account that the probability of observation depends upon the transit probability and the efficiency of the detection. The estimator of the frequency is, $$n_p = \frac{N_p}{\sum_{j=1}^{N_*}p_j}$$ $$ = \frac{N_p}{N_*} \frac{1}{|p|}$$ $$ = \frac{N_p}{N_*^{eff}} $$ The constant \(p_j\) denotes the planetary detection efficiency for the \(j^{th}\) star. As the detection effeciency can deviate and flactuate this is taken care in the equation as it is averaged over the detection efficincy. Using a simplified bayesian model this can be calculated using the likelihood of the planet detection as Gamma distribution. The constant \(\alpha_0\) and \(\beta_0\) taken as the shape parametr and rate parameter. The statistical sample is given by, $$P(\tilde{n_p}|N_p,N_*^{eff})\propto n_p^{\alpha_0 +N_p -1}e^{-n_p(\beta_0 + N_*^{eff})} $$ . This is used in the study for calculating frequency of planet in the period-radius parameter plane. Sample for the statistic analysis was taken from the Kepler mission. In this sample the planets having a radius in the range from the jupyter to earth and period of 1 year is available. In the data cleaning process the data with signal to noise ratio below 7.0 , those planets which is marked as false positive, the planets with radius less than 20 times radius of earth, P greater than 700 days and impact parameter greater than 1 has been removed. the final sample size of the planet distribution is 2,525.

One of the discoveries in the planet statistics is the radius valley; it shows a decrease in the detection of the planet in the radius plane separating the super Earth and the sub Neptune. The theories that are used to explain the radius valley are photoevaporation which could have driven the mass loss and core-powered mass loss but these theories are not without flaws. This is visible in the Kepler survey dine in Zhu and Dong (2021). The dependence of the radius valley to the orbital period and stellar mass has been parameterized as, $$\frac{R_p}{R_p^{valley}}= (\frac{P}{10 days}^g)(\frac{M_*}{M_\odot})^b $$

Conclusion

The study of exoplanet statistics is paving way in understanding and formulating theories on the planet formation, evolution and their distribution. Through the review of literature and results published recently the new constraints on the theory and limitations of the study was explored. The occurrence rate was calculated using the Kepler mission database, some of the data that was ambiguous was removed and using bayesian statistics the frequency distribution of planets in the period-radius plane was mapped and analyzed.
